Global Childhood Immunization Shows Modest Progress Amid Conflict and Growing Vaccine Hesitancy, WHO-UNICEF Report Warns

www.who.int · Wed, 15 Jul 2026 00:01:54 Z

A new WHO-UNICEF report reveals that global childhood immunization coverage in 2025 crept forward but remained below pre-pandemic levels due to conflict, funding cuts, and vaccine hesitancy.

In 2025, global childhood immunization coverage showed modest progress, with 90 percent of infants—nearly 116 million—receiving at least one dose of the diphtheria, tetanus, and pertussis (DTP) vaccine, and 85 percent completing the full three-dose series. According to the latest WHO-UNICEF Estimates of National Immunization Coverage (WUENIC), while these figures represent a slight one-percentage-point increase from the previous year, global coverage remains below 2019 levels. An estimated 13.5 million "zero-dose" children did not receive any vaccines during their first year, highlighting a persistent gap despite a reduction of nearly 750,000 zero-dose children compared to the prior year.

The report highlights significant regional disparities and structural challenges driving volatility. More than half of all zero-dose children reside in fragile, conflict-affected, or vulnerable settings, where political instability and underfunding severely strain healthcare systems. For instance, while Syria experienced sharp declines in vaccine coverage, Sudan recorded remarkable gains by expanding access despite ongoing conflict. Conversely, in middle- and high-income nations, rising vaccine hesitancy and shifting political commitments have caused coverage to slip. This has particularly impacted measles immunization, where global coverage of the first dose stalled at 84 percent, far below the 95 percent threshold needed to prevent highly contagious outbreaks.

UNICEF and WHO, alongside partners like Gavi, the Vaccine Alliance, are urging governments to take decisive action to get the Immunization Agenda 2030 back on track. Key recommendations include strengthening immunization services in fragile environments, countering health misinformation, and securing sustainable domestic and international funding. Additionally, the agencies warned against declining investments in data systems, which are vital for tracking unvaccinated children. Leaders emphasized that immunization remains one of the most cost-effective and equitable health interventions, stressing that no child should suffer from preventable diseases.
