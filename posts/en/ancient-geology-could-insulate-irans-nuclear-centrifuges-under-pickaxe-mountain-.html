Iran's Pickaxe Mountain Geology May Protect Underground Nuclear Facilities

www.scientificamerican.com · Thu, 23 Jul 2026 21:25:00 +0000

The ancient, highly resilient volcanic rock of Iran's Pickaxe Mountain provides formidable natural protection for deeply buried underground nuclear centrifuges against potential airstrikes.

Iran has positioned its sensitive nuclear centrifuges deep beneath Pickaxe Mountain, known in Persian as Kuh-e Kolang Gaz La, creating a formidable shield against potential foreign military strikes. The unique geological characteristics of this site pose severe challenges to conventional bunker-buster munitions. Consequently, standard aerial bombardment strategies may prove largely ineffective against the deeply buried facilities, rendering the complex one of the most protected nuclear sites in the region.

Geological assessments reveal that Pickaxe Mountain is an integral part of an ancient volcanic arc formed tens of millions of years ago. The mountain consists of extraordinarily dense and tough-to-penetrate igneous and metamorphic rock layers. Unlike softer sedimentary rock formations, this ancient volcanic substrate possesses high structural integrity, enabling it to absorb and disperse the kinetic energy and shockwaves produced by heavy explosive penetrators.

This natural fortification carries profound strategic implications for international security and diplomacy. Western defense analysts and geologists emphasize that utilizing such formidable geological features allows Tehran to safeguard its critical nuclear infrastructure from external interdiction. As diplomatic tensions surrounding Iran's nuclear development persist, the natural insulation provided by ancient Earth history remains a vital component of the country's defense strategy.
