Scientists Discover Novel Bacteria Species in Elusive Pygmy Sperm Whales

www.sciencedaily.com · Tue, 21 Jul 2026 01:10:08 EDT

Researchers have discovered three new Helicobacter bacteria species in pygmy sperm whales, revealing severe stomach damage, though their exact link to whale mortality remains unconfirmed.

Researchers analyzing decades of data and tissue samples from pygmy sperm whale strandings have uncovered three previously unknown species of Helicobacter bacteria residing in the animals' digestive systems. Pygmy sperm whales are among the ocean's most elusive marine mammals, rarely observed in the wild due to their solitary nature, small size, and deep-diving capabilities. Consequently, stranding events provide scientists with rare and valuable opportunities to study the biology, health, and hidden diseases of these deep-sea ocean dwellers.

Upon investigating four infected individuals, marine biologists discovered severe gastrointestinal damage in all of the animals. The affected whales exhibited chronic stomach inflammation, severe ulcers, internal scarring, and co-occurring parasite infestations throughout their digestive tracts. While bacteria from the Helicobacter genus are well-documented causes of stomach ulcers and gastritis in humans and land animals, researchers noted that they could not definitively confirm whether these newly identified strains were the direct cause of the whales' strandings or deaths.

Despite the uncertainty surrounding the cause of mortality, the discovery provides crucial insights into the microbial world of deep-diving cetaceans. Scientists emphasize that identifying these novel pathogens is a key step toward understanding how hidden infections might impact marine wildlife health. Further research will be required to determine how widespread these bacteria are among wild populations and whether environmental changes or stress factors exacerbate these infections in fragile ocean ecosystems.
